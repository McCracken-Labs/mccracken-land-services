The Profession

The Landman's Job Changed. Most of Us Haven't Caught Up.

By Michael McCracken • July 15, 2026 • 3 min read

Title is still part of the work. The larger part now is managing data that keeps traveling through the whole organization long after it leaves your hands.

We need to rethink what this profession actually is. Navigating the complexities of title is still part of it, but the larger part now is managing data.

The days of scribbling notes on a yellow pad, turning in a report, and tossing the pad in the trash are behind us. The information you hand a client keeps traveling after it leaves your hands. A landman works at the front of the upstream segment, where the mineral rights get secured before anyone drills, but the data you produce doesn't stay there. Your ownership determination moves through the whole organization, feeding division order interest, driving how well information gets broken down, and determining the royalty payments that reach landowners through departments you'll never interact with directly.

Too many of us miss what that means. When your data isn't prepared so multiple departments and multiple people can actually use it, you've fallen short of your responsibility to the client.

Think about how much waste gets built into the old way of working. Every new person who touches your data ends up retyping it, reformatting it, or reworking it from scratch. Time gets burned, errors slip in, and the client ends up paying for the same work two and three times over. Data that arrives clean and structured enough to move between systems has become a basic expectation of the job rather than a bonus.

GIS is a good example of why this matters. Title information has largely moved off paper and into spatial systems, and a landman who understands how those platforms ingest and display data can produce work that plugs directly into the tools a client already runs. When you understand how the tract you're running ties back to a section, a unit, and a lease boundary, your report stops being something that sits in a drawer and becomes something the organization can build on. That fluency makes you considerably harder to replace.

Replacement is the conversation nobody wants to have out loud. AI has already reshaped this profession over the past decade, and the next ten years will push those changes a good deal further. Staying relevant means paying attention to where the work is heading instead of relying on what worked fifteen years ago.

Running minerals from one owner to the next, or tracing a leasehold across a chain of conveyances, is no longer enough on its own. You have to be able to move between roles. Leasing one day, title the next, data management and GIS woven through all of it, with a real understanding of how each function feeds the others. A landman whose skill set covers only one narrow task is also the one whose work is easiest to hand off to software.

The people who are still doing this in ten years will be the ones who already see the bigger picture. Our job sits at the front of a data pipeline that runs through the whole organization, and how much value we bring comes down to how well we carry that.
