Information Is the Lifeblood. Now It Has to Be Data.

By Michael McCracken • April 29, 2026 • 2 min read

The yellow pad is gone. The job now is capturing courthouse records so they move cleanly from spreadsheet to database to client, and hold up once AI enters the workflow.

For a landman information has always been the lifeblood of what we do. But the way we handle that information has changed. The days of filling out a yellow legal pad with courthouse notes are long behind us. What matters now is not just getting the information down but formatting it in a way that can actually be used, reused, and shared between multiple sources.

Excel, Google Sheets, and custom databases make this possible. At their best, they are not just tables of numbers or names, they are systems that let you turn courthouse records into a runsheet, lease tracker, status report, division order, or even a database that feeds into mapping software. Organized correctly, the same data can answer a dozen questions without anyone retyping it.

A landman needs to understand how to input data correctly. Dates must be consistent. Names and legal descriptions have to match. Column headers need to be clear. A single blank row can kill the sort feature. Skip these details and the spreadsheet stops being useful. It might look full, but it can't be sorted, filtered, or exported without hours of cleanup.

This is where too many newer landmen fall short, and older landmen unwilling to learn new tools get left behind. Don't treat Excel or Sheets like a notepad, treat it as a data tool. Poor formatting doesn't just slow you down, it makes the whole file almost worthless to the next person. It's not enough to capture the record, you need to capture it so it can be used by the next person and understood by everyone in the chain. And now that AI systems are entering landwork, the same rules apply. If the data isn't clean, those tools can't process it. Inputting information properly reduces the cost of document review all the way down the chain to the title attorney.

Every title chain, lease, and curative document today is part of a bigger workflow. That workflow depends on information being stored and entered correctly as data. Structured properly, it can become reports for management, runsheets for drilling, maps for operations, or databases for long-term storage that reduce future costs. As AI becomes part of those workflows, clean data is the difference between a system that helps you and one that spits garbage back.

The good news is these are learnable skills. Taking a few hours to understand data types, formatting rules, and building a simple database will pay off in every project. A landman who can manage information as data will always stand out.

Landwork is no longer about who can fill the most pages with notes. It's about who can structure information so it moves cleanly from courthouse to spreadsheet, from spreadsheet to database, from database to client, and now through AI systems. The landman who can do these things well is the one who makes himself indispensable.
