A Resume Is a Reflection of the Landman

By Michael McCracken • May 27, 2026 • 1 min read

Title work runs on accuracy. So should the resume. What a clean, current, reachable resume says about how a landman will handle the job.

In landwork, attention to detail isn't just a preference, it's a requirement. Every title chain, every lease, every curative document depends on accuracy. That same standard should apply to the resumes we receive from contract landmen.

Too often, resumes come through with spelling errors, formatting issues, or outdated contact information. Even more frustrating is when references or past employers are listed but can't be reached. Hiring managers don't have time to chase down disconnected numbers or hunt for someone who might still be with the company.

If a landman is serious about the work, the resume should reflect it. That means proofreading every section, listing clear and recent work history, and most importantly, providing active, reachable references. It's worth taking the time to verify that old supervisors or HR departments can still be contacted, and that the numbers on the resume are current. A quick phone call on the front end can make all the difference.

When reviewing candidates, we look for people who make our job easier, not harder. A resume that's clean, accurate, and complete speaks volumes about how a landman will handle actual work in the field. If the basics aren't right, it's fair to question how they'll manage complex title or leasing tasks.

In short, a resume is more than a list of past work. It's a reflection of a landman's mindset and habits. Make it count.
